the timeless art of pottery

ceramics throughout the centuries

  • Origins in Prehistoric Times: Ceramics date back over 10,000 years, with early evidence of fired clay objects like the Venus of Dolní Věstonice (29,000 BCE) and functional vessels used by Neolithic communities for storage and cooking.
  • Advancements in Ancient Civilizations: Cultures such as Mesopotamia, Egypt, and the Indus Valley developed pottery techniques, creating wheel-thrown and hand-built forms for practical and ceremonial use. Glazing emerged as a way to seal porous surfaces and add decorative elements.
  • Chinese Porcelain Revolution: During the Tang (618–907 CE) and Song (960–1279 CE) Dynasties, Chinese artisans perfected porcelain production, creating thin, translucent ceramics that were highly valued worldwide. This innovation influenced trade and set the gold standard for fine ceramics.
  • Greek and Roman Innovations: In ancient Greece, black-figure and red-figure pottery became prominent for storytelling through painted scenes. Romans advanced ceramic technology with durable bricks, tiles, and amphorae used in construction and trade.
  • Medieval and Islamic Ceramics: Islamic pottery from the 8th century onward featured vibrant glazes, intricate patterns, and techniques like lusterware, which spread through cultural exchanges with Europe and Asia.
  • European Renaissance and Industrial Era: During the Renaissance, European ceramics incorporated styles inspired by Asian imports. The 18th century saw the rise of mass production with the invention of industrial pottery processes, like Josiah Wedgwood's refined techniques in England.
  • 20th-Century Modern Ceramics: The Arts and Crafts Movement and mid-century modern design brought a revival of handmade pottery, emphasizing artistic expression. Studio pottery flourished, with artists like Bernard Leach championing the fusion of tradition and innovation.
